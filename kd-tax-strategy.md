## Key Concepts

### S-Corp Tax Advantage

Default SMLLC: all net profit hits Schedule C and is subject to ~15.3% self-employment tax (Social Security 12.4% + Medicare 2.9%) up to the SS wage base, plus 2.9%+ Medicare above it.

With S-corp election: profit splits into (a) W-2 salary subject to FICA and (b) distributions **not** subject to SE/FICA tax. The spread between reasonable comp and total profit is the savings.

### Reasonable Compensation

IRS requires S-corp owner-employees to take a "reasonable salary" before distributions. Paying too little is the #1 S-corp audit trigger — IRS can reclassify distributions as wages and impose back payroll taxes + penalties.

**Reasonable-comp study:** a formal written analysis defending the salary number. **RCReports** is the dominant software — CPAs feed in role, hours, duties, location, and practice data; it produces a defensible range based on BLS and proprietary wage data. Alternatives include manual BLS/MGMA benchmarking or Willis Towers Watson surveys. The deliverable is a PDF report kept in the tax file as audit protection.

### QBI — Qualified Business Income Deduction (§199A)

TCJA-era deduction allowing pass-through business owners to deduct up to **20% of qualified business income** on their personal return.

**Threshold is based on *taxable income* on the 1040** (line 15, before QBI deduction itself) — not gross revenue, not net business income. It's household taxable income from all sources (K-1, W-2, investment income, capital gains, spouse's earnings) minus standard/itemized deduction.

Currently scheduled to sunset end of 2025 unless Congress extends.

### SSTB — Specified Service Trade or Business

A carve-out in the QBI rules for "reputation/skill-based" service businesses: **health (dentistry included)**, law, accounting, consulting, financial services, athletics, performing arts, etc.

If you're an SSTB, the 20% QBI deduction **phases out** above income thresholds:
- 2024 MFJ: phaseout runs $383,900–$483,900 (half for single)
- Above the top of the range: SSTB gets **zero** QBI deduction

A successful dentist is almost always fully phased out. Investment income, capital gains, and spouse's outside earnings all push toward the threshold — it's not just KD's profit that determines this.

### SALT — State and Local Tax Deduction (Cap)

When itemizing, you can deduct state + local income taxes and property taxes on your federal return — but TCJA capped the deduction at **$10,000 per household per year**. High earners in tax states easily exceed this (e.g., $40K+ in combined state income + property tax, only $10K deductible). Also scheduled to sunset end of 2025.

### PTET — Pass-Through Entity Tax (SALT Workaround)

Most states adopted this after the IRS blessed it in 2020. Instead of state income tax being paid by the owner personally (where it hits the $10K SALT cap), the **S-corp itself pays the state income tax at the entity level** as a deductible business expense. This reduces federal K-1 income dollar-for-dollar, effectively bypassing the SALT cap. The owner gets a credit on their state personal return for the tax the entity already paid, avoiding double-payment.

Mechanics vary by state (election deadlines, estimated-payment rules, annual opt-in). For an S-corp dentist in a state with income tax, this is typically free money.

## Current Thinking (High Confidence — For Cj's Review)

### 1. Salary vs. Distribution Split

Set W-2 at the **floor** of:
- Defensible reasonable compensation, AND
- The level needed to optimize retirement contributions (Solo 401(k) employer match is computed off W-2 wages)

Take everything else as distributions. Get a reasonable-comp study (RCReports or equivalent) on file if one doesn't exist.

### 2. Erinn's W-2

Size to the **minimum** that unlocks the benefits we actually want:
- Her retirement contribution space (Solo 401(k) / SEP)
- Potential §105 HRA for family medical expenses

Every extra dollar beyond that costs 15.3% FICA with no offsetting benefit (same household, same 1040).

Document her role + hours.

### 3. Kids on Payroll

Put kids on for legitimate, age-appropriate work (modeling for marketing materials, etc.) up to the standard deduction (~$14,600 in 2024, ~$15,000 in 2025), federal income tax free. Use wages to fund **Roth IRAs** ($7,000/yr or earned income, whichever is less) — decades of tax-free compounding.

Shifts income from ~37% bracket to 0%.

Requirements:
- Real work, age-appropriate
- Documented: timesheets, job descriptions, W-2, reasonable hourly rate
- Actually paid into an account in the child's name
- Keep marketing artifacts (photos, materials) as evidence of work product

**Note:** under an S-corp, the parent-employer FICA exemption does **not** apply (that's sole prop / spousal partnership only). See open question below about family management LLC workaround.

### 4. QBI Phaseout

Assume fully phased out as an SSTB at current income level. Confirm with Cj. If phased out, salary-level decisions don't need to factor QBI at all.
